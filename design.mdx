## 1. Product overview: Octopus

Octopus is a **multi‑tenant points automation and reporting platform** for **aggregators** (fish‑game distributors / cafes / online operators), not for players and not for casinos.

Core purpose:

- Automate **loading points** to gaming platforms (FireKirin, Golden Dragon, Orion Stars, etc.) after an aggregator has already taken payment from a player using a gambling‑compatible payment gateway.\[1\]\[2\]\[3\]\[4\]\[5\]\[6\]

- Automate **redeeming points** from those platforms back into the aggregator’s accounts (so they can then pay players using their own gateways).  

- Automate **transfers of points** between game platforms via an aggregator’s distributor accounts.  

- Automate **loaning points** (fronting) based on aggregator‑defined loan rules.  

- Track all these operations in a central ledger and calculate **Octopus’ fees** (monthly \+ per transaction) at the aggregator level, without touching player funds or becoming a payment processor.

Key principles:

- Aggregators are merchant of record with their gambling payment vendors; Octopus never handles player deposits or payouts.  

- Aggregators own KYC, AML, licensing, and gambling regulations; contracts explicitly indemnify Octopus.  

- Octopus operates at the **points layer**, with detailed rules enforcement and reporting, plus **B2B billing** to aggregators.

## 2. Payment infrastructure: only gambling‑friendly tools

Octopus will recommend and integrate with **casino/iGaming payment gateways** for aggregators, and use a **separate B2B billing rail** for SaaS fees.

### 2.1 Aggregator payment vendors (for player deposits/payouts)

These are examples of gateways/processors that **explicitly support gambling/iGaming** merchants:\[2\]\[3\]\[4\]\[5\]\[6\]\[7\]\[1\]

Fiat and e‑wallet gateways:

- Nuvei – global iGaming payment gateway supporting gambling merchant categories, many local methods and cards.\[3\]\[5\]\[7\]

- Paysafe / PaysafeCard – prepaid cards and e‑wallet heavily used in casinos and sports betting.\[5\]\[6\]\[1\]\[2\]

- Skrill – long‑standing e‑wallet for gambling, widely accepted.\[4\]\[7\]\[2\]\[5\]

- Neteller – similar to Skrill; heavily used for casinos.\[7\]\[2\]\[4\]\[5\]

- MuchBetter, MiFinity – wallets designed with casino use in mind.\[2\]\[4\]\[5\]\[7\]

Crypto gateways for casinos:

- CoinsPaid – crypto gateway and processor purpose‑built for online casinos.\[8\]\[9\]\[10\]\[11\]

- PayRam – self‑hosted crypto gateway for iGaming, explicitly designed to avoid frozen accounts and high fees.\[12\]

- NOWPayments, CoinGate – crypto gateways with iGaming integrations and settlement options.\[9\]\[10\]\[11\]\[8\]

Payment orchestration for gambling:

- NexaPay (example like in “Best Casino Payment Gateway” guides) – routes player card/wallet deposits via USDT/USDC settlement, explicitly advertised to online casinos.\[13\]\[14\]

- TRUEiGTECH’s modular payment gateway – built around sweepstakes/iGaming, combining fiat and crypto.\[5\]

Octopus’ role:

- Octopus **does not integrate directly with every PSP**; instead, aggregators either:  

  - Integrate their frontends with these gateways; or  

  - Use a casino payment orchestrator (e.g., NexaPay), and Octopus consumes their transaction summaries via API.

### 2.2 Octopus’ own billing rail (monthly SaaS fees)

Monthly and annual fees Octopus charges aggregators are **pure SaaS billing**, not gambling. Because this is B2B software, you can use:

- A SaaS/B2B billing provider like Paddle, Maxio, or a B2B‑focused Stripe alternative that **allows high‑risk merchants for B2B services** (not gambling deposits).\[15\]\[16\]\[17\]\[18\]

To stay strictly aligned with your requirement:

- Do **not** use Stripe for anything that could be construed as “gambling‑related processing” – use a billing provider that explicitly supports high‑risk B2B SaaS where your clients are gambling companies.\[3\]\[7\]\[13\]

Octopus billing: billed **upfront**, via this B2B billing provider, with no reliance on aggregators’ own gambling gateways.

## 3. Business model: fees and pricing

### 3.1 Monthly SaaS fee (prepaid like a phone plan)

Octopus offers volume‑based tiers:

Starter:

- Up to 2,000 automated transactions/month.  

- One “frontend bundle” (e.g., one online site or one cafe).  

- Price: about \$300/month, prepaid.

Growth:

- 2,001–10,000 transactions/month.  

- Up to three frontends in one bundle (e.g., website \+ Facebook \+ Instagram, or online \+ two cafes). One of the three is effectively “free” (3 for price of 2).  

- Price: about \$600/month, prepaid.

Scale:

- 10,001–50,000 transactions/month.  

- Up to six frontends/locations, with similar bundle logic.  

- Price: about \$1,000/month, prepaid.

Annual option:

- Prepay 12 months, get the 13th month free (13 months access for 12× monthly price).

This aligns with common SaaS pricing benchmarks (tiered by volume and feature) and your desire to charge like a phone plan: pay up front, then use the platform.\[17\]\[18\]\[19\]

### 3.2 Per‑transaction fees (deducted in real time from aggregator’s earnings)

Octopus charges per automation event, not per bet:

Transaction types:

- Load (points credited to distributor).  

- Redeem (points debited from distributor and converted to aggregator payouts).  

- Transfer (points moved between distributors via aggregator wallet).  

- Loan (points fronted to player).

Your fee structure:

1. Flat fee per transaction  

   - \$0.50 per **redeem** and per **loan issuance**, since these are high‑value events where aggregator earnings are real.  

   - Optionally \$0.25 per **load** and **transfer** if you want to monetize those but keep them cheaper.

2. 20% on aggregator commission fees (non‑negotiable)  

   - For any transaction where the aggregator charges a “commission fee” (e.g., 10% of winnings, 10% on cashouts), Octopus takes **20% of that commission**.

3. 50% on other aggregator earnings (negotiable floor 35%)  

   - “Other earnings” include:  

     - Interest on loans.  

     - Their share of gross winnings (e.g., they keep 50% of total win).  

     - Excess winnings kept due to caps (e.g., win \$600, redeem capped at \$200, aggregator keeps \$400 as excess points/cash).  

   - Octopus default: **50% of these other earnings** per transaction.  

   - Negotiable lower bound: **no less than 35%** for aggregators you want to incentivize.

Implementation:

- On each redeem/loan transaction, the aggregator’s system (or Octopus if you host this logic) calculates:  

  - `aggregator_commission_amount`  

  - `aggregator_interest_amount`  

  - `aggregator_excess_amount` (e.g., value of excess points).  

- Octopus calculates the fee:  

  - `fixed_fee` (e.g., \$0.50)  

  - `fee_on_commission = aggregator_commission_amount * 0.20`  

  - `fee_on_other = aggregator_other_earnings * rate`, where `rate` defaults to 0.50 but can be negotiated down to ≥0.35.  

  - `total_octopus_fee = fixed_fee + fee_on_commission + fee_on_other`.  

- Octopus expects aggregators to **deduct this fee from their own earnings on that transaction** (commission/interest/excess) in real time, and logs that deduction.

This ensures:

- You are not invoicing per‑transaction fees later.  

- Fees are structurally tied to aggregator’s own earnings, especially punishing those who harshly tax players.

## 4. Octopus data model and rules

Octopus stores data at aggregator, player, distributor and transaction levels.

### 4.1 Aggregator configuration and rules

Aggregator:

- `id`, `name`, `status`, `pricing_tier`.

AggregatorPaymentConfig:

- `aggregator_id`  

- `gateway_type` (Nuvei, Paysafe, Skrill, CoinsPaid, etc.).  

- `gateway_config` (API keys / endpoints, encrypted).  

- Octopus only uses this to sync deposit/redeem records or to validate references; it never initiates player payments.\[6\]\[4\]\[8\]\[9\]\[3\]\[5\]

AggregatorRuleSet (per aggregator):

- `min_redeem_by_distributor`: e.g., `{"firekirin": 50, "golden_dragon": 25}` (values in \$).  

- `max_redeem_multiple_of_last_deposit`: e.g., `10`.  

- `redeem_commission_percent`: e.g., `10`.  

- `transfer_commission_percent`: e.g., `10`.  

- `loan_enabled`: true/false.  

- `loan_min_amount`, `loan_max_amount`, `loan_interest_percent`, `loan_repayment_rules`.  

- `excess_handling`: how excess is treated (e.g., aggregator keeps all excess points).

These rules are owned by aggregators; Octopus stores and enforces them as part of points automation, not as regulatory rules.

### 4.2 Player and distributor accounts

Player:

- `id` (Octopus internal).  

- `external_ids` (optional mapping to aggregator’s player IDs).

PlayerAggregatorAccount:

- `player_id`, `aggregator_id`, optional aggregator‑specific metadata.

PlayerDistributorAccount:

- `player_id`, `aggregator_id`, `distributor_id`.  

- `external_distributor_username` or `account_id`.  

- `points_balance`.  

- This supports FireKirin’s multi‑account model: aggregators may shift players between sub‑accounts; Octopus updates these mappings as part of account management workflows.

### 4.3 Transaction logs

LoadTransaction:

- `id`, `aggregator_id`, `player_id`, `distributor_id`.  

- `points_added`.  

- `cash_amount` (optional).  

- `deposit_reference` (ID from gambling gateway).  

- `initiated_by` (always “aggregator”).  

- `platform_fee` (e.g., \$0.25).  

- `timestamp`.

RedeemTransaction:

- `id`, `aggregator_id`, `player_id`, `distributor_id`.  

- `total_points_removed`.  

- `commission_points`.  

- `net_points_to_player`.  

- `cash_amount` (optional).  

- `aggregator_commission_amount`.  

- `aggregator_other_earnings` (interest, share, excess).  

- `octopus_fee_components` (fixed, commission %, other %).  

- `payout_reference` (aggregator’s payout ID from Nuvei/Skrill/etc.).  

- `initiated_by` (“aggregator”).  

- `rules_snapshot`.  

- `timestamp`.

TransferTransaction:

- `id`, `aggregator_id`, `player_id`.  

- `source_distributor_id`, `target_distributor_id`.  

- `points_requested`.  

- `commission_points`, `net_points_transferred`.  

- `platform_fee`.  

- `timestamp`.

LoanTransaction:

- `id`, `aggregator_id`, `player_id`, `distributor_id`.  

- `points_loaned`.  

- `loan_terms_snapshot` (interest %, repayment period).  

- `aggregator_interest_amount`.  

- `octopus_fee_components`.  

- `platform_fee`.  

- `timestamp`.

AggregatorFeeLedger:

- `aggregator_id`, `period`.  

- Aggregated counts per transaction type.  

- Summed `total_platform_fees`.  

- Subscription status.

This gives complete visibility for Octopus without storing player deposits/payouts, only references.

## 5. End‑to‑end workflows (detailed, from deposit to support)

### 5.1 Load points (player deposit → points at distributor)

1. Player pays aggregator via gambling gateway (Nuvei, Skrill, CoinsPaid, etc.).  

   - Aggregator is merchant of record; gateway approves or rejects.\[10\]\[1\]\[6\]\[8\]\[9\]\[3\]\[5\]

2. Aggregator maps deposit to points: \$50 → 50,000 points.  

   - Records last deposit amount for that player/distributor.

3. Aggregator calls Octopus `loadPoints` API with:  

   - `aggregator_id`, `player_id`, `distributor_id`, `points`, `deposit_reference`, optional `cash_amount`.

4. Octopus validates aggregator, player, distributor; logs LoadTransaction and platform fee.  

5. Octopus calls distributor’s API/panel to credit points (FireKirin sub‑account, etc.).  

6. Octopus updates PlayerDistributorAccount and returns success to aggregator.

Octopus does not initiate or receive the deposit; it operates solely on points.

### 5.2 Redeem points (player withdrawal → points out, aggregator pays player)

1. Player asks aggregator to redeem points (winnings).  

   - Aggregator uses its own UI/frontends (web, Facebook, Instagram, cafe).

2. Aggregator applies its own rules: min redeem, max 10× last deposit, commission %, excess handling.  

   - Calculates `player_payout_amount`, `aggregator_commission_amount`, `aggregator_other_earnings` (including excess if capped).

3. Aggregator calls Octopus `redeemPoints` API with full detail.  

4. Octopus:

   - Validates rules against AggregatorRuleSet.  

   - Validates aggregator earnings numbers.  

   - Calculates Octopus fee: \$0.50 fixed \+ 20% of commission \+ 50% (≥35% negotiable) of other earnings.  

   - Logs RedeemTransaction and fee.

5. Octopus calls distributor’s API/panel to debit points.  

   - Updates PlayerDistributorAccount.

6. Aggregator uses its gambling gateway to pay the player (card/e‑wallet/crypto).  

   - Aggregator subtracts Octopus fee from its own commission/earnings during this calculation.

7. Aggregator optionally calls Octopus to attach payout\_reference.

Octopus never initiates the payout; it only ensures rules and fees are applied at the points and aggregator‑earnings level.

### 5.3 Transfers between game platforms (A → aggregator → B)

1. Player asks aggregator to move points between distributors.  

2. Aggregator calls `transferPoints`.  

3. Octopus validates, calculates transfer commission, logs TransferTransaction, debits A, credits B via distributor APIs.  

4. No cash moves; aggregator sees updated points.

### 5.4 Loans (fronting points)

1. Player requests a loan; aggregator decides and sets terms.  

2. Aggregator calls `loanPoints` with points and loan\_terms\_snapshot.  

3. Octopus validates against loan rules, computes Octopus fee as \$0.50 \+ % of interest, logs LoanTransaction.  

4. Octopus credits points via distributor API.

Repayment flows use aggregator’s gateways; Octopus logs follow‑up transactions if aggregator sends them.

## 6. UI and roles: you, aggregators, sub‑accounts, cafes

### 6.1 Octopus admin (your UI)

You need a simple admin PWA:

- View aggregators, their pricing tier, usage stats and fees.  

- Configure global defaults for fees (fixed amounts, commission %, other earnings %).  

- Monitor “underpaid” aggregators (those not applying your transaction fees correctly, via reconciliation).  

- Launch support tasks and AI agents (see next section).

Built via:

- No‑code tools (Bubble/WeWeb) or Next.js front end on top of Supabase/Xano.

### 6.2 Aggregator admin

Aggregator frontends:

- Web admin where aggregators:  

  - Configure payment gateways (Nuvei, Skrill, etc.).  

  - Configure rules (min redeem, max 10×, commissions, loan terms).  

  - Manage sub‑accounts for distributors (FireKirin sub‑accounts).  

  - See detailed transaction logs and fees paid to Octopus.  

- Octopus must support aggregator’s constant creation and mapping of distributor sub‑accounts:  

  - API/UI for “create new PlayerDistributorAccount”, “reassign player to new sub‑account”, “sync balances”.

### 6.3 Cafe physical load

For cafes:

- Aggregator staff receive cash from players.  

- Staff use aggregator’s own UI to mark cash deposits and call Octopus `loadPoints` with `cash_source = "physical_cafe"`.  

- Octopus treats this like any other load event, but tagged for physical reporting.

## 7. Build, deployment, support with agentic AI

You want:

- No‑code or low‑code build.  

- Generative/agentic AI for admin, verification, analysis, support, without adding latency to critical paths.

### 7.1 Build stack

Backend:

- Supabase or Xano for multi‑tenant data and APIs.  

- Choose one; both can be configured quickly.

Frontends:

- Aggregator admin PWA (Bubble/Next.js).  

- Octopus admin PWA.

AI agents for build/ops:

- Use workflow automation and AI agent frameworks:  

  - n8n, LangChain, CrewAI for orchestrating data checks, pricing optimizations, and rule audits.\[20\]\[21\]\[22\]\[23\]\[24\]

### 7.2 Agentic AI usage

Examples, kept off the critical transaction path:

- **Pricing optimization agent**: continuously reviews aggregator usage and suggests new tier structures; doesn’t block transactions.\[21\]\[23\]\[24\]\[20\]

- **Reporting & anomaly detection agent**: scans transaction logs for unusual patterns (e.g., aggregators not applying your fees correctly, unusual excess usage) and flags them; doesn’t add latency to point load/redeem.\[23\]\[24\]

- **Support agent**: handles aggregator questions, explains fee breakdowns, guides them through rule setup.

Rules:

- Agents must **not** be in the synchronous automation path for `loadPoints`, `redeemPoints`, etc.  

- Agents run asynchronously on logs and metadata, so they can’t slow down point transfers.
